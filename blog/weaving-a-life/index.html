Weaving a Life

Having discussed the figure of death - Giltinė, I would like to cover a slightly more positive deity - Laima. If to simply speak,Laima is a goddess that represents life and anything that is connected with it (it can be fertility, destiny and even luck). In Lithuanian mythology death and life are both connected through their sisterhood and the everlasting circle that repeats itself ad infinitum. Death and life are the two inseparable figures due to their familial and functional ties. In the grand scheme of things the goddesses of life and death balance each other out.

Despite the aforementioned sisterhood, the deity of life - Laima - was one of the foremost and eldest goddesses in the Lithuanian pagan pantheon. She was the intercessor between the Earth and Heaven, thus, in a way this goddess was viewed as an ultimate goddess, whose significance rivalled that of Saulė. Laima was a motherly figure who embodied fertility, justice, fate and even light. The whole description of this goddess may seem pretty vague as there are plenty of other sacred figures that possess similar powers. However, we should understand that this goddess was primarily a weaver of life: she determined the fates of human lives and, therefore, human morality.

Despite the major role that this goddess played in representing fertility (specifically birth as opposed to the harvest), I think that she should also be recognised as a goddess of justice. Overall, the main function of this goddess was to assist men in their lives and to assure their well-being. Having said that, Laima was considered more as a goddess judging the life of men rather than a goddess representing the actual fertility aspect in the human life (as there is a separate goddess for this in Lithuanian mythology).

Lithuanian mythology featured a lot of resemblances to aspects of nature, so it is not inappropriate to say that the goddess Laima was imagined as a zoomorphic creature: in a form of various birds, such as swans, cuckoos and ducks. This particular selection of birds reveals more about this divinity and how the Lithuanian people understood their deities through references to nature. As in many mythologies, the bird was a symbol of lightness, freedom and good things in general.

It is important to mention that the cult of this goddess developed from the practices of totems and the belief in spirits. In the beginning of this cult (Palaeolithic Age), Laima was highly associated with various cosmic objects as wells as the idea of confronting light and darkness. These associations were the foundation of the Lithuanian pagan thought.

It is interesting to see how these thoughts have changed over time and how the cult of Laima resembling the matriarchal hierarchy shifted to a more simplistic practice. So, the later cult of this goddess appears to carry on: she is considered to be a representing deity of life and destiny. From the Palaeolithic thought, she continues her function as the bearing divinity of the beginning and meaning (as the cosmic being).

It is also fascinating how the image of Laima as an ultimate goddess of life shifts into a more simplistic being when the cult of a "christianised" god of thunder emerges. He then overtakes the main responsibility of this deity and thenceforth controls the fate of the human life and the morality of men. In a sense Laima is one of the divinities that were sadly replaced by the arrival of the Indoeuropeans and their patriarchal system in mythology. However, she was not completely erased, she was after all viewed as a goddess controlling the birth of human beings. In addition to that, the deity of life was later associated with the witches, thus the name Laumė, who overtook so many features that Laima was bearing.

On the other hand, the cult of this goddess became very personalised. Laima was like a key goddess to women in terms of giving advice and guiding them in the most important events of their lives. Even the Lithuanian people became Christians, Laima was imagined as the Virgin Mary fostering all women and appearing to them as the heavenly assistant. The goddess would heal, test people, she would later more and more appear to an individual person to manifest her presence. Eventually, people were imagining that this goddess lived in a forest and that her another symbol was a lime tree.

So, from an imagine of a life weaver, Laima was transformed into a very human like divinity that assisted in every aspect of a woman's life. From being regarded as a cosmic being, Laima was turned into a very domestic creature, so earthly and yet so diverse.
